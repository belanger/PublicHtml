Material:

This course will cover the derivative, along with some interpretations and applications; the integral, along with some interpretations and applications; the connection between the two, and higher dimensional versions of the derivative and integral.

Grading criteria:

(See the note on solutions below.)
The grading will be based on the following:

  • Whether or not the solutions are written the way people read; left to right, top to bottom.
  • Whether or not you've identified everything you've written down.
  • Whether or not you've used notation correctly.
  • Whether or not you've justified what you've written down.
  • Whether or not you've written the answer at the end, as the conclusion, clearly identified.
  • Correctness.

Solutions:

When solving a problem, be sure to understand what you are being asked and be sure to answer the question explicitly and include any necessary units. Be sure to write up your solution clearly and appropriately. This basically means: Understand what is being asked, understand what you are writing down, and don't be unnecessarily lazy. Keep in mind:

  • The solution to a math problem isn't simply a result, but rather the process of getting to the proper result.
  • All necessary steps should be justified.
  • Make clear whether your statements are assumptions or are conclusions that follow from previous statements. Use words, as appropriate. For example, instead of \begin{center} $x=1, y = 2, x + y = 3$ \end{center} you should write \begin{center} Since $x=1, y = 2$, we know $x + y = 3$ \end{center}
  • Do not include unnecessary information.
  • While using scratch paper is part of solving math problems, your solutions are not scratch work, they are written for other people to read.
  • Solutions should be written the way that most people read; left to right, top to bottom. The writing should not jump around the page.
  • Everything should be identified.
    (Never write an isolated expression; if you want to indicate that x is equal to eight, you shouldn't just write 8 or even x   8, you should write x = 8.)
  • Proper mathematical notation should be used; that means knowing what the symbols you write down mean, and (for example) using equal signs where they belong (and not using them where they don't belong). Solving the equation x3=8 by a string of equations such as x3=8=x=2 is incorrect, since x cannot be equal to both 8 and 2. Similarly, computing 23 by writing 23     8 is incorrect, since an equal sign is missing.
  • Using words is often helpful and encouraged. Don't use symbols from logic, such as ∴, ⇒ and ∀. Use words instead. Arrows should not be used outside of their use in limits.
  • The numerical answer should appear at the end of your solution, explicitly identified. If you are solving for x and determine it to be 5, the bottom of your work should have x=5.
A solution which is not properly written is not correct.

Tests and Quizzes:

There will be tests on the days listed above. They will consist of five problems, worth 10 points each. There will be a quiz on most weeks when there isn't a test. The quizzes will consist of one problem, worth 10 points. At the end of the semester, only the quizzes whose percentage is above your average test percentage will be counted toward your grade. For tests and quizzes, you can bring a calculator and standard pen or pencil. The calculator must be an actual calculator (as opposed to a phone app). It can be a graphing calculator, but not one that does symbolic manipulation (like a TI-89 or TI-92). Note that if you are expected to justify a result, simply referring to the calculator is not sufficient. The tests and quizzes will not be made up of homework problems, but they will be based on the homework problems. Thus, the best way to learn the material is to understand how to do the homework problems. You are expected to keep up with the work, not cram before a test. I will not discuss several sections at once, and I will not answer questions about tests and quizzes on the days they are to be given.

Academic Dishonesty:

You know the difference between right and wrong. Be honest, don't cheat. On tests and quizzes, you must do your work yourself without looking at other people's work or using any equipment or material not explicitly allowed. You must finish the test by the end of the class. Anyone caught cheating will have their grade affected, and may even fail the class.
